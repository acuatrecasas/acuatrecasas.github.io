The second day my wife and I arrived in Raleigh, we saw it painted on a wall. Four words. We stopped. We looked at each other. We didn't need to say anything.

*Too small to fail.*

I've been carrying those words since. This is my attempt to explain why.

*BTW — I'm writing here for the same reason I do most things: I need structure to understand, and I need to understand to remember. This is my corner — outside my head — to shape thoughts, ask questions, and share things worth the time.*

Years ago, Rolls-Royce came to my university to interview students. I signed up. The morning came. I walked to the building, reached the door — and turned around.

Not because of the interview. Because of what I'd already decided: my grades weren't good enough, my English wasn't good enough, I wasn't enough. The failure hadn't happened yet, but I'd written the story before walking in. So I left before it could prove me right — or wrong.

That moment marked me more than any failure ever has. Because I didn't fail. I didn't even try. And that, I realized, is the only real failure. I made a decision that day: never again. Not because I'd suddenly become braver, but because I understood something about fear: it makes doors look like walls.

Which brings me back to those four words — and to the big questions we've all asked.

Who are we, really? What is our role here?

You can approach these questions from science, from faith, from philosophy. None of them give you a final answer. Not because the answer doesn't exist, but because it's different for each of us. Reality itself works this way. My wife and I have stood in the same places, lived the same moments, and walked away with different truths. We don't even share the same version of the years we've spent together.

But some things are shared. And one of them is this: we are small. Not as an insult. As a fact. And as an invitation.

Small in space: one person, one city, one planet, one galaxy among hundreds of billions.

Small in time: humanity's story — everything we've built, loved, destroyed, and discovered — is a footnote in the life of the universe.

Humility isn't weakness. It's accuracy. It's seeing yourself at the right scale.

And when you do — something shifts. Because if we are that small, our failures are small too. What feels like a catastrophic mistake — a wrong turn, a bad bet, a door you walked away from — is, at the scale of everything, just an attempt.

The more you try, the more you fail. But also: the more you try, the more you learn, grow, and close the distance between where you are and where you want to be.

This is what Too Small To Fail means to me. Not a consolation for when things go wrong. A reminder that failure is smaller than it looks. And trying is bigger than it feels.

I walked away from that door at twenty. I've walked through a lot of doors since. Not because I stopped being afraid — but because I understood that the fear and I are both very, very small.

And something that small can't afford not to try.
