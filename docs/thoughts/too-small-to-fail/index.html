Too Small To Fail

Jun 23, 2026· Alejandro W. Cuatrecasas Schmitz

The second day my wife and I arrived in Raleigh, we saw it painted on a wall. Four words. We stopped. We looked at each other. We didn’t need to say anything.

Too small to fail.

I’ve been carrying those words since. This is my attempt to explain why.

BTW: I’m writing here for the same reason I do most things: I need structure to understand, and I need to understand to remember. This is my corner outside my head — a place to shape thoughts, ask questions, and share things worth the time. This is my first post.

Years ago, Rolls-Royce came to my university to interview students. I signed up. The morning came. I walked to the building, reached the door — and turned around.

Not because of the interview. Because of what I’d already decided: my grades weren’t good enough, my English wasn’t good enough, I wasn’t enough. The failure hadn’t happened yet, but I’d written the story before walking in. So I left before it could prove me right — or wrong.

That moment marked me more than any failure ever has. Because I didn’t fail. I didn’t even try. And that, I realized, is the only real failure.

I made a decision that day: never again. Not because I’d suddenly become braver, but because I understood something about fear.

It makes doors look like walls.

Maybe that is why those four words stayed with me. Too small to fail.

At first, they sound almost contradictory. We spend so much of life trying not to feel small. We want to matter. We want to be capable, respected, certain, ready. We want our decisions to feel important, but not so important that we can mess up everything.

And still, some of the questions that matter most tend to make us feel small.

Who are we, really? What is our role here?

You can approach those questions through science, through faith, through philosophy. None of them has ever given me a simple answer. But all of them have given me perspective.

My wife and I can stand in the same places, live the same moments, and walk away with different truths. Even the years we have spent together live differently in each of us. Reality is shared, but our experience of it never fully is.

And yet, some things are shared.

One of them is this: we are small. Not as an insult. As a fact. And as an invitation.

Small in space: one person, one city, one planet, one galaxy among hundreds of billions.

Small in time: humanity’s story — everything we’ve built, loved, destroyed, and discovered — is a footnote in the life of the universe.

Humility isn’t weakness. It’s accuracy. It’s seeing yourself at the right scale.

And when you do, something shifts. Because if we are that small, our failures are small too. What feels like a catastrophic mistake — a wrong turn, a bad bet, a door you walked away from — is, at the scale of everything, just an attempt.

That doesn’t make failure pleasant. It doesn’t mean it won’t hurt, or cost something, or stay with you longer than you expected. But it does make failure less absolute.

The more you try, the more you fail. But also: the more you try, the more you learn, grow, and close the distance between where you are and where you want to be.

I walked away from that door at twenty. I’ve walked through a lot of doors since. Not because I stopped being afraid — but because I learned to see fear at the right scale.

This is what Too Small To Fail means to me: failure is smaller than it looks, and trying is bigger than it feels.
